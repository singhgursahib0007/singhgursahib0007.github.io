Legal Pluralism and Indigenous Legal Orders in Canada

(Wet'suwet'en / Coastal GasLink Pipeline)

1.1 3-MINUTE OPENING SUMMARY 3 MIN

"Hi everyone. Today we’re talking about how different legal systems can live together in one country. In Canada, we already do this with English and French laws. But there’s a third system that often gets ignored: Indigenous Legal Orders."

"Back in 2021, a pipeline was started through Wet'suwet'en land. This land was never given away in a treaty, so the people there still have their own rights. The traditional leaders, the Hereditary Chiefs, said 'no' to the pipeline, but the government moved forward anyway. When people tried to protect the land, the courts used a standard Canadian order to bring in the police. This created a direct clash between two different sets of laws."

"Here’s the thing: the Wet'suwet'en have their own way of solving problems. It’s called the Feasthouse. It’s a formal legal process with ceremony, where everyone gets a voice and decisions are made together for the good of the land. It’s not just a meeting; it’s a living legal system with its own rules."

"Canada has promised to follow UNDRIP, which says Indigenous people must give 'free and clear consent' before projects like this start. That didn't happen here. Even though British Columbia passed laws to follow these rules, they still used force to stop the protectors."

"So the real question is: why do we treat one legal system as 'real' law and the other as something that can just be pushed aside? It’s not about who has the most power; it’s about being a fair country that actually keeps its own promises."

1.2 ETHICAL ANALYSIS

From a Utilitarian view, respecting Indigenous laws is the right choice because it prevents costly court battles and keeps the peace, creating the most happiness and stability for everyone in Canada. At the same time, Cultural Relativism reminds us that Indigenous nations have their own legal traditions that should be judged by their own standards, not just forced to follow Western rules. By honoring these different ways of knowing, we build a more fair and respectful country that values all cultures equally.

1.3 1-MINUTE QUICK RESEARCH 1 MIN

The Case Study

Coastal GasLink built a long pipeline (670-km) across Wet'suwet'en land. This land was never given away. The elected band councils (who run the reserves) agreed because of the money and jobs. But the traditional leaders, called Hereditary Chiefs (who look after the whole territory), said no. The government chose to listen to the elected councils and used the police and courts to finish the project by late 2023.

The Possible Solution

The best way forward is fixing how decisions are made for the future. The government should have followed the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP) to obtain free and clear consent before starting:

  • Legal Recognition: The government should officially recognize the authority of Hereditary Chiefs over traditional lands to ensure clear legal rules for future projects.
  • UNDRIP Consent: Officials must follow United Nations rules by obtaining free and clear consent. This ensures that no project starts without a shared agreement.
  • Joint Management: The government and Indigenous nations should share power. This allows the Nation to help design projects and set rules before construction begins.

1.4 PROFESSOR'S Q&A (1-MINUTE)

Q1: "Moral reasons for including ILOs and compatibility with common/civil law?"

It is morally right to include Indigenous laws because Canada already accepts two other colonial legal systems; it’s only fair to include the third for Indigenous history. They are compatible because both aim for justice and order. The Feasthouse is a public, structured process just like a courtroom, only it uses consensus and ceremony to reach its goals.

Q2: "What role does upholding ILOs play in implementing UNDRIP?"

Upholding Indigenous laws is the only way to actually make UNDRIP work. You cannot have "informed consent" if the government ignores the legal system the community actually uses to make decisions. By recognizing these laws, the government finally gives Indigenous people a real seat at the table instead of just forcing rules upon them.

Q3: "Obligation of non-Indigenous Canadians when protectors are stopped by police?"

Our obligation is to hold the government to its own promises. If the government passes human rights laws like UNDRIP but then uses police force to stop the very rights those laws protect, citizens should speak up. We have a duty to support fair negotiation over force and ensure our country follows its own words.

1.5 OPPONENT INTERACTION

Appreciation Points

  • "Your team made a strong point about the importance of legal consistency, and that is a real concern worth engaging with seriously."
  • "We appreciate that your team took the complexity of UNDRIP implementation seriously. Harmonizing legal systems is genuinely difficult, and acknowledging that difficulty is a good starting point."

Critical Questions

  1. "Canada already recognizes two distinct legal traditions under the Constitution. What principled argument can be made for stopping at two? What is the actual threshold for a legal system to deserve recognition?"
  2. "British Columbia passed legislation to implement UNDRIP in 2021, and a court issued an injunction against Wet'suwet'en protectors in the same year. How do you explain that contradiction, and who bears responsibility for it?"
  3. "Is there a meaningful difference between a court proceeding and a Feasthouse proceeding in terms of their ability to produce a just outcome? If yes, what makes one more legitimate than the other, and who gets to decide?"
  4. "UNDRIP requires free, prior, and informed consent. That consent was not obtained before this pipeline was approved. Does that mean the approval was ethically valid?"
  5. "When non-Indigenous Canadians see the state use police force to shut down an Indigenous legal process, what obligation do they have to respond?"

Anticipated Criticisms & Responses

Criticism Our Response
"Indigenous Legal Orders are too vague to be enforceable in a national system." Vagueness is not unique to Indigenous systems. Common law itself evolves through precedent because it has to adapt to new situations over time. John Borrows documents ILOs as systematic and sophisticated. The vagueness concern tends to be applied selectively to Indigenous systems and not to others.
"Recognizing ILOs would create legal chaos and uncertainty." New Zealand's experience with Maori legal principles and the Whanganui River decision shows that integration is possible and manageable. The realistic outcome is a transition framework, not chaos.
"The elected band council approved the pipeline, so Indigenous consent was given." Elected band councils derive their authority from the Indian Act, which is a colonial statute. Hereditary Chiefs hold authority under Indigenous Legal Orders over unceded territory. Using one structure to override the other does not satisfy the requirement of free, prior, and informed consent.
"Economic development benefits Indigenous communities as well." Development imposed without consent is not a benefit. It is extraction. Genuine benefit requires consent, and consent requires recognizing who holds the legitimate authority to give it.

45s

45s

30s

Cell Phone Bans in Schools

(Ring, Ring, Ring, Ban-anaphone!)

2.1 3-MINUTE OPENING SUMMARY 3 MIN

"Hello. We’ve all seen the news lately about schools banning cell phones. From California to New York, districts are passing new rules to stop students from using phones during the day. This isn't just a small trend; it's happening everywhere because people are worried about how technology affects learning."

"The reasons for these bans make sense on the surface. Teachers say phones are a huge distraction. Studies show that when students put their phones away, they focus better, get higher grades, and there’s less bullying. Even the U.S. Surgeon General has warned that social media is hurting the mental health of young people."

"But the science isn't actually settled. Other experts point out that many of these mental health issues might be from the stress of the pandemic, not just from phones. Plus, phones are a part of real life now. If we just ban them, are we actually teaching kids how to use them responsibly?"

"There's also a big fairness issue. For some students, a smartphone is their only way to get online for homework. Other students need them for medical reasons or to stay safe. A total ban can hurt the students who are already struggling the most."

"So, the question isn't just 'are phones bad?' The real question is how we make rules that are fair and based on actual facts. A simple ban might seem like an easy fix, but it's much more complicated than that."

2.2 ETHICAL ANALYSIS

Social Contract Theory suggests that we follow school rules like cell phone limits because we have an agreement to work together for a better learning environment that benefits everyone. However, Subjective Relativism tells us that morality is personal; for a student who relies on their phone for safety or learning, a total ban might feel like a personal harm. A fair policy finds balance by keeping the "contract" of a focused classroom while respecting the individual needs of every student.

2.3 1-MINUTE QUICK RESEARCH 1 MIN

Schools use different ways to make sure students follow the rules. Some use "Yondr Pouches," which are magnetic bags that lock the phone until the student leaves school. Others use locked bins or cubbies where phones are kept during class. Some schools simply have an "Off and Away" rule, where students keep their phones turned off in their own backpacks or lockers.

Most of these rules have important exceptions to keep students safe. For example, students with medical conditions like diabetes can keep their phones to check their health. Teachers can also allow phones for specific lessons. Most importantly, schools must have a way for parents to reach their children in an emergency, and students with special learning needs (IEPs) are allowed to use technology that helps them learn.

A big study from Norway in 2024 showed that strict phone rules helped students, especially girls, get better grades. It also helped reduce bullying for everyone. This is important, but we have to remember that what works in one country might not work the same way everywhere else. We should think carefully before making one big rule for every single school.

Another study from a school in London looked at phone bans and found that they helped students who were struggling the most. Students who were already doing well didn't see much change. This suggests that instead of banning phones for the whole day, we could just limit them during class time to help the students who need it most.

Internal papers from Facebook show that the company knew its apps like Instagram were hurting teenagers' mental health, but they didn't do anything about it. These apps are designed to be as addictive as a slot machine to keep people using them. This means the problem isn't just that students can't stop using their phones; it's that the apps are built to make it almost impossible to put them down.

2.4 PROFESSOR'S Q&A (1-MINUTE)

Q1: "Does phone use impact academic outcomes like learning and cheating?"

Phones cause distraction and make cheating easier, which definitely hurts learning. However, they are also tools for research. The best answer isn't a total ban, but a clear rule that limits phones during class while teaching students how to use them for work when needed.

Q2: "Does mobile phone use at school impact students’ mental health and wellbeing?"

Research shows a link between heavy phone use and higher anxiety or lower wellbeing. Apps are designed to keep students hooked, which can be bad for their mental health. Schools should create spaces where students can unplug and focus on their real-world connections.

Q3: "Does phone use at school impact rates of cyberbullying?"

Yes, having constant access to social media at school can make it easier for bullying to continue throughout the day. A school policy that restricts phones can give students a break from online drama and help reduce the chances of bullying during school hours.

Q4: "Is it ethical to ban mobile phones in schools?"

It is ethical to restrict phones during lessons to help everyone focus. But a total, all-day ban is often unethical because it ignores students who need phones for health, for safety, or because it's their only computer. A fair policy is flexible and protects the rights of those who need them most.

2.5 OPPONENT INTERACTION

Appreciation Points

  • "Your team correctly identified the real and documented harm that phone distraction causes in classrooms. That concern is legitimate and the research behind it deserves to be taken seriously."
  • "We agree that the mental health of students is a pressing concern and that schools have a responsibility to address it. The disagreement is about whether a blanket ban is the most ethical and effective way to do that."

Critical Questions

  1. "If the causal link between phone use and mental health harm is still scientifically contested, does that uncertainty justify a blanket ban, or does it call for more targeted and evidence-based intervention?"
  2. "For students from lower income families who rely on smartphones as their only digital device, how does your proposed policy avoid making their situation worse rather than better?"
  3. "Social media platforms are deliberately engineered to be addictive using variable reward mechanisms. Should the ethical burden for that harm fall on students and schools, or on the companies that designed these products? Why?"
  4. "At what point does student autonomy become relevant in this debate? High school students are approaching adulthood and will live in a world where these devices are everywhere. Does removing phones from school help them navigate that world or not?"
  5. "At the start of 2010, ninety one percent of schools had phone bans. By 2016, that number dropped to sixty six percent as phones became more integrated into daily life. What evidence suggests that reversing this trend will produce lasting results rather than another policy pendulum swing?"

Anticipated Criticisms & Responses

Criticism Our Response
"The Norwegian and NYIT studies prove that bans work." Those studies show correlation in specific contexts and populations. They do not establish universal causation. Good policy requires replicable evidence across diverse settings before restricting student rights at scale.
"Children cannot self-regulate, so schools must act." This framing puts the burden entirely on students while ignoring the structural problem. Students are contending with products designed to override self-regulation. The failure is partly a design ethics failure, not only an individual one.
"Bans are already showing results in California and the UK." The evidence is still early and limited. Long-term effects, equity impacts, and implementation differences have not been fully evaluated. Short-term improvements for some students do not prove that blanket bans are the right policy for all students.
"Paternalism toward minors is clearly justified." Justified paternalism still requires proportionality. The least restrictive means of achieving a legitimate educational goal is more ethical than a broad prohibition. A policy that restricts phones during instruction with clear accessibility exceptions is proportionate. An all-day ban without accommodations is not.

2.6 3-MINUTE REBUTTAL DEFENSE 3 MIN

30s

Acknowledge the real evidence supporting phone restrictions and the genuine concern about addictive technology.

45s

Pivot to the equity argument: bans without accommodation disproportionately harm the students who already have the least support.

45s

Challenge causality: distinguish between correlation and evidence strong enough to justify restricting student rights at scale.

30s

Reframe responsibility: point to the Facebook Papers and addictive design as evidence that the ethical burden should fall partly on technology companies, not only on students and schools.

30s

Propose a more proportionate alternative: evidence-based, context-sensitive policies with clear equity accommodations and teacher-level discretion.

JUDGES Q&A PREPARATION

11 Strategic Responses

Q1

"How do you distinguish legitimate legal pluralism from legal fragmentation that weakens the rule of law?"

Legal pluralism does not mean that all legal systems apply equally in all contexts at all times. It means that more than one system is recognized and that they interact through structured frameworks. Canada already manages this between common law and civil law by defining which system applies in which jurisdiction. The same kind of framework can be built for Indigenous Legal Orders. The rule of law is only threatened when legal systems operate without accountability or transparency. The Feasthouse is neither of those things. It is a public, structured, and procedurally consistent process. Legal fragmentation comes from refusing to engage, not from recognizing that more than one tradition exists.

Q2

"UNDRIP is not legally binding at the international level. Does that weaken the argument for recognizing ILOs?"

The international status of UNDRIP does not determine its domestic force. Canada incorporated UNDRIP through the Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples Act in British Columbia in 2021, which makes it a domestic legal commitment in that province. Beyond the legal question, moral obligations are not contingent on whether a treaty is formally binding. The moral case for free, prior, and informed consent stands on its own. If Canada has said it will implement these rights, then the failure to do so in the Coastal GasLink case is both a legal and a moral inconsistency.

Q3

"Could recognizing ILOs set a precedent for other community-based legal systems to override Canadian law?"

This concern conflates distinct legal categories. Indigenous Legal Orders are grounded in constitutionally recognized Aboriginal title and in the historical fact of prior sovereignty over specific territories. They are not voluntary associations based on shared belief. The legal basis for recognizing ILOs is territorial and historical, not cultural preference. A principled distinction can be drawn, and it already exists within Aboriginal law. Recognizing ILOs does not open the door to every community claiming its own legal jurisdiction.

Q4

"The elected band council approved the pipeline. Does that not constitute Indigenous consent?"

Elected band councils derive their authority from the Indian Act, which is a colonial statute designed to replace traditional governance with a system more compatible with federal control. Hereditary Chiefs hold authority under Indigenous Legal Orders over unceded territory. These are two different governance structures with different sources of legitimacy. Using a colonial governance structure to authorize activity that traditional authorities oppose does not satisfy the standard of free, prior, and informed consent. It is circular reasoning: you cannot use the instrument of colonialism to demonstrate that colonial processes have been consented to.

Q5

"Is a blanket phone ban ever ethically justified, or are you arguing for no restrictions at all?"

The argument here is not against all restrictions. Restricting phone use during instructional time is pedagogically and ethically defensible, and the evidence supports it for certain student populations. The argument is against blanket, all-day bans that are applied without accessibility accommodations, without equity analysis, and without considering the needs of the students who are most at risk of harm from over-restriction. The ethical test is whether the policy uses the least restrictive means to achieve a legitimate educational goal. During class, the answer to that test can support a ban. For the entire school day without exceptions, it usually does not.

Q6

"If social media companies are the real problem, why should school policy be the focus?"

Both levers should be used at the same time. Holding technology companies accountable through legislation, design standards, and algorithmic transparency requirements is an important and underused tool. School policy is not an alternative to that. It is an immediate response to a present harm in a specific setting. However, schools should not be asked to carry the entire moral weight for a problem that was created upstream by design decisions made by technology companies. The ACM Code of Ethics says that computing professionals have a duty to avoid harm. Social media platforms that use addictive design on adolescents are in violation of that principle, and the ethical conversation should include them.

Q7

"What do you say to teachers who describe phone use as a serious obstacle to teaching every day?"

That experience is real and it deserves to be taken seriously. A finding that one third of public school teachers describe phone use as a major problem is significant data. The response to that problem should be evidence-based, graduated, and designed with both teacher autonomy and student equity in mind. Teachers should be empowered to set context-specific policies in their own classrooms, rather than having broad state-level bans handed down from above. The concern is valid. The question is whether a blunt legislative instrument is the most ethical and effective way to address it.

Q8

"How do you weigh the rights of the majority of students who are distracted against the minority who rely on phones for accessibility?"

Utilitarian majorities do not override fundamental rights. Accessibility is protected under disability law in most jurisdictions, and policies that remove accommodations without providing alternatives may be legally questionable as well as ethically problematic. Beyond formal disability accommodations, digital equity is a broader concern. For students in lower income households, a smartphone may be their only computing device for homework, research, and staying connected. A good policy centers the students with the least institutional support, not those with the most. The presence of majority distraction does not justify policy that harms students who already face the greatest barriers.

Q9

"Does virtue ethics support bans to build attentiveness, or inclusion to build digital responsibility?"

Virtue ethics produces a genuine tension here rather than a clean answer, and that tension is productive. Both attentiveness and digital literacy are real virtues that students need in order to flourish. The question is which institutional environment is most likely to cultivate both over the long term. A school that simply removes phones may produce students who are focused in class but underprepared for a world built around these devices. A school that teaches structured and purposeful phone use, knowing when to engage and when to put it away, may cultivate a deeper form of practical wisdom. Aristotle called this phronesis. It is the capacity to judge what is appropriate in a given situation, and it is the capacity we should be building.

Q10

"Is there a risk that treating Indigenous Legal Orders as worthy of recognition obscures their internal diversity or conflicts with individual rights?"

Indigenous Legal Orders are not monolithic. They vary across nations, they have evolved over time, and they are subject to internal debate, just like every other legal system. The argument is not that ILOs are perfect or uniform. It is that they deserve recognition and engagement on equal terms with other legal traditions. The Feasthouse is notable because it is explicitly inclusive. It invites outside voices and dissenting perspectives. Concerns about individual rights within ILOs should be addressed through the same respectful and bilateral engagement that is being called for here. Those concerns should not be used as a reason to deny recognition to an entire category of legal tradition.

Q11

"How do computing ethics principles apply directly to the cell phone case?"

There are three direct applications. First, the ACM Code of Ethics states that computing professionals have an obligation to avoid harm, and that obligation extends to how systems are designed for vulnerable populations. Social media platforms that use variable reward mechanisms to keep adolescents engaged are violating that principle at the design level. Second, value-sensitive design, which is a framework developed within computing ethics, holds that systems should be designed with the values and wellbeing of all affected stakeholders in mind. Designing for maximum engagement without regard for the cognitive and emotional wellbeing of young users is a design failure, not just a social one. Third, digital equity is a core concern in computing ethics, and any policy that restricts digital access must include an analysis of who bears the cost of that restriction and whether those costs are distributed fairly.

ETHICAL THEORIES QUICK-REFERENCE

Relativism Theories

Subjective Relativism

Morality is just based on the individual. What is right for you is whatever you personally believe is right.

Cultural Relativism

Right and wrong should be judged by the standards of the culture they come from, not by outside rules.

Moral Relativism

The idea that being "right" depends entirely on cultural norms or what a specific person believes.

Epistemic Relativism

The belief that even "truth" or "knowledge" depends on your specific perspective or language.

Theological and Self-Interest Theories

Divine Command Theory

Something is right if God commands it and wrong if God forbids it. Morality is based on God's will.

Ethical Egoism

An action is right if it helps the person doing it. Doing what is best for yourself is the priority.

Consequence-Based Theories

Act Utilitarianism

Look at each individual action. The right choice is the one that creates the most happiness for everyone involved.

Rule Utilitarianism

Follow rules that would create the most happiness for the most people if everyone followed them all the time.

Duty and Rule-Based Theories

Kantianism (Deontology)

Focus on duty. Actions must follow universal laws (rules that should apply to everyone). Never use people just as a "means to an end."

Social and Character-Based Theories

Social Contract Theory

Morality is an agreement between people to follow rules so we can all live together safely and happily.

Virtue Ethics

Focus on character. An action is right if it's what a good, virtuous person would do in that situation.

Care Ethics

Focus on relationships, trust, and compassion. It’s about understanding and helping others rather than just following rules.

REAL-WORLD SUPPORTING CASE STUDIES

Tsilhqot'in Nation v. British Columbia (2014)

The Land Rights Precedent

Situation: The Supreme Court of Canada issued its first ruling recognizing Aboriginal title over a specific territory.

Ethical Dilemma: Can Indigenous land rights take precedence over provincial resource extraction decisions?

Relevance: Establishes that Indigenous territorial authority is legally recognized at the highest court level in Canada. Strengthens the argument that Wet'suwet'en authority over unceded land is grounded in existing Canadian jurisprudence.

Whanganui River Legal Personhood, New Zealand (2017)

The Integration Model

Situation: The New Zealand Parliament granted the Whanganui River legal personhood based on the Maori understanding of the river as an ancestor.

Ethical Dilemma: Can Indigenous ways of understanding the natural world be incorporated into a national legal framework?

Relevance: Proof that legal systems can be redesigned to accommodate Indigenous worldviews without producing chaos. Directly counters the argument that recognizing ILOs is impractical.

UK School Phone Ban Study, LSE (2023)

The Equity Impact

Situation: Researchers at the London School of Economics evaluated the effects of phone bans across schools in England.

Ethical Dilemma: Do bans improve outcomes equitably, or do they benefit some students at the expense of others?

Relevance: Found the strongest positive effects for the lowest-achieving students. Supports the argument for targeted, equity-aware restrictions during instruction while raising questions about all-day blanket bans.

Jonathan Haidt versus Candice Odgers (2024)

The Evidence Debate

Situation: The publication of The Anxious Generation sparked a major academic debate about whether phones and social media cause harm to adolescent mental health.

Ethical Dilemma: Should policy act on contested evidence, or wait for stronger certainty before restricting student rights?

Relevance: Illustrates the tension between the precautionary principle and the standard of evidence-based policy. Both positions are ethically serious, and the disagreement is about what level of evidence justifies action.

The Facebook Papers (2021)

The Design Failure

Situation: Leaked internal research from Meta showed that the company knew Instagram was causing harm to teenage girls and chose not to act on that knowledge for commercial reasons.

Ethical Dilemma: Do technology companies have an ethical obligation to prioritize user wellbeing over engagement and profit?

Relevance: Directly implicates computing ethics. The harm associated with phone use in schools is partly the result of deliberate design choices made against user wellbeing. This shifts part of the moral responsibility from students and schools to the companies that built these platforms.
