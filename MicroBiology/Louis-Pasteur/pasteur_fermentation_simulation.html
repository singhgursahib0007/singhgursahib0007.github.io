What this simulation encodes
  • Yeast use glycolysis → fermentation to convert sugar into CO₂ and ethanol under anaerobic conditions.
  • At low sugar, metabolism is limited → few bubbles, slower ethanol formation.
  • At optimal sugar, yeast are well-fed → dense bubbles and clear alcohol production.
  • At very high sugar, osmotic stress reduces activity: yeast struggle despite abundant substrate, showing that "more sugar" is not always better.
  • Pasteur showed that fermentation is a biological process carried out by living cells, not just a spontaneous chemical decomposition.
Try these quick experiments
  1. Set sugar to 10–15%: Mild activity Compare the sparse bubbles to Pasteur's weaker fermentation in poor media.
  2. Click "Optimal": Vigorous CO₂ Strong ethanol signal mirrors living-yeast-driven fermentation.
  3. Push sugar to 80–100%: Osmotic stress Note how yeast motion and bubbles diminish despite excess substrate.

Use the visuals plus the readouts above: if bubbles and ethanol stay low, the system is metabolically quiet—even with sugar present. This distinction underlines Pasteur's conclusion that fermentation requires viable yeast cells, not just sugar.
